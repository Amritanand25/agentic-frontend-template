# Baby Duck Syndrome in UX

> **Users hate changes.** The tendency of a user to compare each new computer system to that which they originally learned. If a new system or UI is not familiar, the user will likely perceive it negatively.

Also known as the **Phenomenon of Imprinting**, studied in-depth by ethologist Konrad Lorenz.

This can be compared to newly hatched ducks or other birds who get attached to the first creature they see in their life and consider it their mom. Similarly, users "imprint" on the first interface they learn and judge all subsequent changes against that original experience.

---

## What is Baby Duck Syndrome?

How to explain it when your users reject any new significant changes in UI? The users tend to stick with the current UI and if there is something new unfamiliar to them, they will likely judge it negatively. This cognitive bias means that even objectively better designs may be initially rejected simply because they feel different.

---

## Why It Matters for Product Teams

- Users may resist redesigns even when the new version is measurably more usable
- Negative feedback after a launch doesn't always mean the design is bad -- it may reflect imprinting bias
- Understanding this syndrome helps teams plan rollouts and set realistic expectations for user reception
- It explains why gradual evolution often succeeds where radical redesigns fail

---

## Practical Tips

- Provide assistance and guide your users through the transition phase.
- Let the UI communicate with your users rather than instructing them to make the change.
- Be certain that the changes are absolutely needed and worth the risk.
- Run user testing and observe how users perceive and interact with the new UI.
- Introduce incremental changes in UI.
- Interact and listen to your users.
